## What Is the F1 Visa and How Does It Differ from Other US Visas?

The F1 visa is specifically for full-time students enrolled at accredited US academic institutions. It is different from:

- **B1/B2 (Tourist visa)** -- You cannot study on a tourist visa.
- **J1 (Exchange visitor)** -- For exchange programmes, often with a two-year home residency requirement.
- **M1 (Vocational student)** -- For vocational or non-academic programmes.
- **F2 (Dependent visa)** -- For spouses and children of F1 holders.

The F1 allows you to study full-time, work on campus (up to 20 hours/week), and access Optional Practical Training (OPT) after graduation -- 12 months for most fields, and 36 months for STEM graduates.

## What Are the Step-by-Step Requirements to Apply for an F1 Visa?

Here is the exact process, in order:

**Step 1: Get admitted to a SEVP-certified school.**
Your US university or college must be certified by the Student and Exchange Visitor Programme. After admission, the school's Designated School Official (DSO) will create your record in SEVIS and issue your I-20 form. This is the most critical document in your F1 application.

**Step 2: Pay the SEVIS fee.**
Before scheduling your visa interview, pay the I-901 SEVIS fee of USD 350 (N542,000) at fmjfee.com. Keep the payment receipt -- you will need it at the embassy.

**Step 3: Complete the DS-160 form.**
The DS-160 is the online nonimmigrant visa application form. Fill it out carefully at ceac.state.gov. You will need:
- Your passport details
- Travel history for the last 5 years
- Education and work history
- US school information (from your I-20)
- A digital passport photograph

Save your DS-160 confirmation page and barcode. You cannot proceed without it.

**Step 4: Pay the MRV visa fee.**
Pay USD 185 (N286,000) through the approved payment channels (currently via the US visa portal and bank deposit). Save your payment receipt.

**Step 5: Schedule your visa interview.**
Book your appointment through the US visa scheduling portal. During peak season (May-August), slots can fill up quickly. Book as early as possible -- ideally 3-4 months before your programme starts.

**Step 6: Gather your supporting documents.**
More on this below.

**Step 7: Attend the visa interview.**
Arrive early. The interview itself typically lasts 2-5 minutes.

<Callout type="tip">
The DS-160 form is long and detailed. Set aside 60-90 minutes to complete it carefully. Use a stable internet connection, save frequently, and double-check every entry against your passport and I-20. Errors on the DS-160 can cause delays or raise red flags during your interview.
</Callout>

## What Documents Do I Need for My F1 Visa Interview?

Organise your documents in a clear folder. The consular officer may or may not look at all of them, but having everything ready demonstrates preparedness.

**Required documents:**
- Valid passport (with at least 6 months validity beyond your intended stay)
- I-20 form (signed by you and the DSO)
- DS-160 confirmation page
- SEVIS fee payment receipt (I-901)
- MRV fee payment receipt
- Passport-sized photograph (if not uploaded digitally)
- Admission letter from your US school

**Financial documents:**
- Bank statements (last 6-12 months) showing sufficient funds
- Sponsor's bank statements and letter of sponsorship (if funded by a parent or relative)
- Scholarship letter (if applicable)
- Tax clearance or evidence of sponsor's income source
- Employment letter of sponsor (showing salary and position)

**Academic documents:**
- WAEC/NECO result
- University transcripts and degree certificate
- IELTS/TOEFL/SAT/GRE score reports
- Professional certifications (if relevant to your programme)

**Ties to Nigeria (evidence you will return):**
- Family ties documentation (marriage certificate, birth certificates of dependants)
- Property documents (Certificate of Occupancy, deed of assignment)
- Business registration documents
- Employment letter (if currently employed, showing approved leave or your role)

## How Do I Pass the F1 Visa Interview in Nigeria?

The F1 interview is where most Nigerian applicants either succeed or fail. The consular officer is evaluating three things:

1. **Are you a genuine student?** -- Do you understand your programme, your school, and your academic goals?
2. **Can you afford it?** -- Are your financial documents credible and sufficient?
3. **Will you return to Nigeria?** -- Do you have strong ties that make it logical for you to come back after your studies?

Here is how to approach the most common questions:

**"Why do you want to study in the US?"**
Be specific. Name the programme, the university, and what makes it unique. "I want to study Computer Science at Arizona State University because their programme includes a specialisation in AI and machine learning, which aligns with my goal of building AI solutions for Nigeria's fintech sector."

**"Why this university?"**
Show that you researched your school. Mention specific professors, labs, rankings, or programme features. Avoid generic answers like "it is a good school."

**"Who is paying for your education?"**
State clearly: "My father/sponsor is funding my education. He is a [profession] and has been saving for this over the past [X] years." Be prepared to explain the source of funds in your bank statements.

**"What will you do after graduation?"**
This is the critical question. The officer wants to hear that you plan to return to Nigeria. Say something like: "After completing my Masters, I plan to return to Nigeria to work in [specific industry]. Nigeria's [sector] is growing rapidly and my US education will give me a competitive advantage." Even if you intend to use OPT, frame it as gaining experience to bring back home.

## What Are the Most Common Reasons for F1 Visa Denial?

Section 214(b) is the most common refusal ground. It means the officer was not convinced that you are a genuine temporary visitor. Specific triggers include:

- **Weak ties to Nigeria** -- If you are single, unemployed, and have no property or business in Nigeria, the officer may doubt you will return. Strengthen your ties before applying.
- **Inconsistent financial documents** -- Sudden large deposits, unexplained income sources, or bank statements that do not match the sponsor's declared salary. Your financial narrative must be consistent and verifiable.
- **Poor interview performance** -- Nervousness is normal, but contradicting your DS-160 answers, being vague about your programme, or appearing rehearsed can all raise doubts.
- **Programme does not match background** -- An accountant applying for a degree in agricultural science, or a doctor applying for a community college diploma, will face tough questions.
- **Previously overstayed or had visa issues** -- Any immigration violations in the US or other countries will complicate your application.

<Callout type="warning">
Never lie during your visa interview. Consular officers are trained to detect inconsistencies, and they have access to your previous visa applications and travel history. A single lie -- about your sponsor's income, your travel history, or your intentions -- can result in permanent visa ineligibility. Always tell the truth and let your genuine story speak for itself.
</Callout>

## What Happens After My F1 Visa Is Approved?

Once your visa is stamped in your passport:

1. **Book your flight.** You can enter the US up to 30 days before your programme start date.
2. **Arrange housing.** Contact your school's international office for dormitory options or off-campus housing guidance.
3. **Attend orientation.** Most schools require a mandatory international student orientation in the first week.
4. **Maintain your status.** Once in the US, you must attend classes full-time, maintain good academic standing, and not work off-campus without authorisation. Violating these conditions can result in losing your F1 status.

## Can I Work in the US on an F1 Visa?

Employment options for F1 students include:

- **On-campus employment** -- Up to 20 hours/week during school, full-time during breaks. No special authorisation needed.
- **Curricular Practical Training (CPT)** -- Work related to your field of study, authorised by your DSO. Available during your programme.
- **Optional Practical Training (OPT)** -- 12 months of work authorisation after graduation. STEM graduates can extend to 36 months.
- **Economic hardship employment** -- Available in cases of unexpected financial difficulty, with USCIS approval.

OPT is the primary reason many Nigerian students choose the USA. Three years of post-graduation work experience in a STEM field -- earning USD 50,000-80,000 per year (N77M-N124M) -- can be transformative for your career and finances. More details on the [USA destination page](/destinations/usa).
